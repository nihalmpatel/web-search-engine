$6M for bike lanes, trails to build on 'tremendous growth' in cycling across Essex County

Sharon Hill, Windsor Star
Updated: March 22, 2019
Share Adjust Comment Print

“Tremendous growth” in cycling on Essex County’s roads and multi-use trails made spending another $6 million on even more routes an easy choice this week.

County council on Wednesday unanimously approved adding $3.7 million worth of bike lane and pathway projects to its County Wide Active Transportation System. That’s on top of the $2.26 million already approved in the county’s budget for rural paved shoulder projects.

“It’s creating a whole new industry for us which is cycling tourism,” Essex County Warden Gary McNamara said Friday. “We’re starting to connect the dots, so to speak, and eventually we want to connect the whole county and all the trail systems.”

John Scott, a Windsor cyclist who is the chairperson of the Share the Road Cycling Coalition in Ontario, emailed a letter to every county council member congratulating them on the investment.

The former Town of Essex councillor remembers when wineries and other businesses started pushing for bike lanes years ago along County Road 50. Now, businesses such as Farm Dog Cycles and WindsorEats regularly sell out as a growing number of cycling tourists visit the wine route.

“It became more than just a bunch of guys in spandex that wanted bike lanes,” Scott said Friday.

A July count conducted at 66 spots across the County Wide Active Transportation System, called CWATS for short, found 57 per cent of the users were cyclists. Only four per cent of them were considered elite or advanced cyclists. There were families, recreational cyclists and migrant workers using the signed routes.

It’s a great investment and now it’s really starting to gel

The 102 hours of observational counts found 755 people used the system: 427 cyclists and 328 pedestrians. In both 2017 and 2018, the number of people on bicycles exceeded those on foot for the first time since 2015.

“Cycling, in particular, has seen tremendous growth; between 2013 and 2018, the number of daily bike trips doubled,” said a report to council.

The if-you-build-it-they-will-come cliche is unavoidable for McNamara. Once the county starting creating safer opportunities to walk or cycle to the nearest park, to work or to multi-use trails such as the Chrysler Canada Greenway, the usage increased, he said.

“We certainly feel it’s a great investment and now it’s really starting to gel,” McNamara said.

CWATS is an $80-million, 20-year plan to create 800 kilometres of multi-use trails, bike lanes and paved shoulders that will connect all seven county municipalities. So far about $20 million has been spent.

The system links to the Herb Gray Parkway Trail so cyclists from Windsor (and eventually Detroit when the international crossing is finished) can get to the Chrysler Canada Greenway and associated trails to reach Leamington and the southern end of the county without travelling on a road.

The $3.7 million of construction approved Wednesday means almost 10 kilometres of bike lanes, paved shoulders and multi-use trails will be added this year.

The largest projects are the $1.45-million, 3.6-km paved shoulder route along County Road 50 from Evergreen Road to County Road 23 in southern Essex and the $1.35-million multi-use trail and paved shoulder in Lakeshore on County Road 2 from Wallace Line to Patillo Road.

There’s three short sections of bike lanes being done in Leamington on Talbot Road and County Road 20 and a one-km bike lane in Amherstburg on Sandwich Street between Lowes Sideroad and Pickering Drive.

Tecumseh will get a multi-use trail link on County Road 11 from North Talbot Road to Highway 401 and Kingsville is continuing to add room for cyclists on County Road 50 with a paved shoulder project near Ford Road.

Benefitting local municipalities pay 60 per cent of the $3.7-million cost.

The county covers the $2.26 million of rural paved shoulder projects since it does them with road improvements.

So far, CWATS is a 400-km system, but more than half of it is long-distance signed routes awaiting upgrades. There are 83 km of multi-use trails, 87 km of paved shoulders, 7.9 km of separated cycle paths and 4.2 km of dedicated bike lanes, according to a report to council.
