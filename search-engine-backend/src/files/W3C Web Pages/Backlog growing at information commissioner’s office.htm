Backlog growing at information commissioner's office

D'Arcy Hande, the researcher who triggered the Pinehouse inquiry, says the backlog is a major problem.

Alex MacPherson, Saskatoon StarPhoenix
Updated: December 2, 2019
Share Adjust Comment Print

D’Arcy Hande is worried about the files piling up on the desk of Saskatchewan’s information and privacy commissioner.

Hande, a retired provincial archivist whose repeated requests for records from the Northern Village of Pinehouse triggered a sweeping government inquiry, said Ron Kruzeniski’s office must be “snowed under.”

That appears to be the case. Earlier this year, while straining to meet the 120-calendar-day target for issuing review reports, the Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner received a $107,000 funding increase, bringing its annual budget to $1.97 million.

While $80,000 of that was earmarked for a new analyst, bringing the total to seven, it did not make a major dent in the growing backlog of files awaiting review. Last year, Kruzeniski’s office opened 301 new files; this year, it is projecting 390 — 40 more than the record.

“Obviously, the information commissioner’s office needs more staff. It’s that simple,” Hande said.

“It’s a big problem. Transparency and accountability are the essence of democratic government. If citizens can’t get answers, we’re in the dark — we don’t know how decisions are being made by our governing authorities.”

Kruzeniski is well aware of the situation. Under ideal circumstances, he said, each of the seven analysts would have 15 files open at any given time. Some currently have more than 40 on their desks, and it’s not unthinkable that the forecast of 390 new files could be low.

“For people who want that information, it’s not good,” Kruzeniski said, adding that the current wait time for his office to review a privacy breach or a disputed freedom of information request is around 190 days — more than six months.

“That’s just not performance,” he said.

Under provincial law, anyone can request records from a host of public institutions. The list of eligible “local authorities” includes the provincial government and its agencies, plus Crown corporations, universities, municipalities large and small and police forces.

Many requests are submitted by private individuals like Hande, but they are also frequently used by journalists and Opposition political parties seeking information about the inner workings of different organizations and levels of government.

Kruzeniski attributes the sharp spike in requests and reviews to multiple factors, including high-profile data breaches, journalists’ efforts to obtain government records and broader public awareness inspired by Hande’s Pinehouse probe.

While it is obviously good that more people are aware of their ability to access public information, Kruzeniski said, it comes with consequences, including frustration for applicants, public bodies and the general availability of information.

“If something happens in November that has a democratic implication or issue to it, my issuing a report next November — the story has come and gone and come and gone and maybe come and gone again,” he said.

Hande is far from the only person worried about the implications of long wait times for information. While the Canadian Taxpayers Federation rarely advocates for more spending, it is prepared to make an exception in this case.

“Accountability saves taxpayers money in the long run,” CTF Prairie Director Todd MacKay said, adding that in his view the review process should generally take “weeks and months, not months and years.”

MacKay said the government should change its culture to favour proactive disclosure — putting documentation online before anyone decides to ask for it. There is an incentive to do just that, he said.

Instead of having damaging information come out one freedom of information request at a time — which in cases such as the Global Transportation Hub scandal can prolong bad news for years — it’s easier just to be transparent, he said.

“It’s a little bit like when I tell my kids when they break something, ‘Just be honest and tell me.’ It’s hard for them to believe they’re going to get in less trouble when they’re honest, but that’s the simple reality.”

Hande took a slightly different view. Speaking generally, he said any government that has been in power for years accumulates “baggage,” and since there is little incentive to reveal it, the tendency is to slow down the process.

Not only should the office have order-making powers — as Kruzeniski has recommended — but it needs more resources, Hande said, adding that he is “very concerned” about any organization that does not want to be accountable.

Kruzeniski is about a month away from his annual presentation to the Board of Internal Economy, the bipartisan legislative committee that controls funding for his office and those of other independent officers, such as the provincial auditor.

While he could ask for funding for seven new analysts — which would make sense given Hande’s view that his office is about 100 per cent over capacity — the commissioner said he will likely ask for two more so as not to be deemed “unreasonable.”

“That would certainly bring down our numbers. Would it bring it right down to the 15 (files per analyst)? I don’t know. I know if I ask for funding to double the number of analysts, I just wouldn’t get it,” he said.

“There’s a whole bunch of other good, important things that are crying out for money and you’re competing for resources.”
